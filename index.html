Gyeongbokgung Palace

The grandest of Seoul's Five Grand Palaces, built in 1395 and rebuilt after the Imjin War.

Gyeongbokgung Palace, meaning “Palace Greatly Blessed by Heaven,” was commissioned by King Taejo in 1395 as the principal seat of the Joseon dynasty and symbol of its sovereignty. The palace was razed by Japanese forces during the Imjin War in 1592 and lay in ruins for nearly three centuries before being restored under King Gojong in 1867, with new structures like Geoncheonggung and Taewonjeon added during that period. A comprehensive revival began in the 1990s—culminating most recently in the 2023 restoration of the Gyejodang Hall—returning key sections such as Gwanghwamun Gate, Heungnyemun Gate, and Hyangwonjeong Pavilion to their historic glory. Architecturally, Gyeongbokgung melds grand ceremonial spaces—like the Geunjeongjeon Throne Hall—with serene garden pavilions such as the hexagonal Hyangwonjeong surrounded by a lotus-filled pond, conveying a harmonious balance of power and nature. Today, the palace hosts the National Palace Museum and the Royal Guard Changing Ceremony, drawing millions of visitors annually to experience its layered history and reconstructed beauty.
